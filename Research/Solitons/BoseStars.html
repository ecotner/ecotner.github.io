Boson Stars

  This part of my research is about modeling the gravitational collapse and evolution of cold scalar particles into a "Boson star", and the subsequent evolution and interactions of this type of object. Using these results, I hope to be able to be able to explain experimental data on the distribution of dark matter in galaxies and dark matter self-interaction cross section limits, or apply their interesting properties to cosmological scenarios.

  When created through some non-thermal mechanism (like the Afflec-Dine process, or vacuum realignment in the case of axions), scalar particles form a Bose-Einstein condensate (BEC), which can be treated as a macroscopic wavefunction. This then allows us to use the Schrodinger equation to solve for the time evolution and equilibrium states of the BEC. The gravitational and self interactions between field makes this equation highly nonlinear and nonlocal (often referred to as the NNSE or Nonlinear Nonlocal Schrodinger Equation), and requires either approximate solutions using the variational method, or numerical codes to solve properly. Their properties are very interesting: it appears that the more massive the condensate, the more tightly it is bound, and so heavy boson stars are actually incredibly dense. Some even believe that they would make good candidates for black hole mimickers, and could explain the supermassive black holes at the center of the Milky Way and other galaxies. Their radius is inversely related to the mass of the scalar field, and so stars made of very light bosons can be very large. Condenstates with particle mass of $10^{-23}$ eV can be hundreds of kiloparsecs across, and could explain why the Galactic nucleus has a dark matter core as opposed to the cusp predicted by CDM simulations.

  I am currently investigating various types of numerical methods to model the time evolution of the BEC collapse into a Bose star, from which we can extract the timescale of collapse, spatial distribution, binding energy, mass, and other properties of this gravitationally bound state.
